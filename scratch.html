The Four Languages I Speak

Languages are not merely tools for communication — they are windows into entirely different worlds of culture, thought, and human experience. Growing up in Ukraine and later living across Central Europe, I came to understand four languages not from textbooks, but from life itself.

  1. Ukrainian — My Native Tongue
    • Spoken since birth in Brovary, Ukraine
    • Language of my family, earliest memories, and deepest emotions
    • Part of the East Slavic family, written in the Cyrillic alphabet
  2. Russian — Acquired Through Immersion
    • Widely co-spoken alongside Ukrainian across many regions
    • Developed strong proficiency through daily conversational use
    • Now teaching Russian as a Teaching Assistant at BYU
  3. Czech — Learned Through Service
    • Learned during 18 months of living in Czech Republic and Slovakia
    • Part of the West Slavic family — a fascinating shift from my Cyrillic roots
    • Key differences I discovered compared to Ukrainian:
      • Uses the Latin alphabet instead of Cyrillic
      • Contains the unique phoneme ř — found in no other language
      • Different grammatical cases and verb aspects
      • Tonal rhythm feels distinctly “Western” in character
  4. English — The Language of Opportunity
    • Developed through dedicated formal study and full immersion in the USA
    • Primary language of my academic coursework at BYU Marriott
    • Bridges all of my multicultural experience to global professional opportunities

My Story

Growing up in Brovary, Ukraine, I never imagined that languages would become one of my greatest strengths. The journey began naturally — Ukrainian at home, Russian in the streets, the ordinary rhythm of Eastern European life.

Everything changed when I had the opportunity to live in Czech Republic and Slovakia for nearly two years. Suddenly I found myself in a world where Slavic grammar felt familiar but every single word was foreign. Czech became my third language not through textbooks, but through thousands of real conversations with real people — in homes, in marketplaces, in community centers, and on cobblestone streets lit gold by the afternoon sun.

Now, studying Business at BYU, I find that multilingualism is not just a personal achievement — it is a professional superpower. Understanding how people think in their own language gives me deeper empathy for customers, colleagues, and communities. It changes the way I listen, the way I market, and the way I lead.

Each language I speak is a passport — not to a country, but to a different way of seeing the world.

Sofiia Pshenychna
BYU Pre-Business · Provo, Utah
